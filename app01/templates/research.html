Research Area

Nano-Devices
Our research is focused on the fundamental understanding of quantum transport and optical excitation phenomena at the nanoscale interfaces based on novel low-dimensional materials such as graphene, nanotubes, quantum dots, and DNA. This can provide the characterization and design of novel "More Moore & More than Moore" nano-electronic, nano-energy, and nano-bio devices.

  • Carbon electronics
  • Molecular electronics
  • Biosensors
  • Spintronic devices
  • Light emitting diodes
  • Solar cells
  • Photocatalysts
Nano-Materials
We are interested in the nanostructured interfaces relevant for advanced electronic, energy, and bio-devices. For this purpose, we develop and apply first-principles and multiscale simulation tools and investigate the atomic, electronic, transport, and optical properties of contacts involving.

  • Carbon nanomaterials: graphene (2D), carbon nanotubes (1D), fullerenes (0D), & their composites
  • Graphene-like 2D materials: hBN, transition metal dichalcogenides (e.g. MoS2, WSe2), etc.
  • Bio- and bioinspired nanomaterials: DNA, porphyrin, etc.
  • Nanostructured (0, 1, & 2D) metal oxides & metal sulfides
  • Self-assembly & 3D architectures
Computations
We are strongly interested in developing and applying novel simulation theory and programs that are required to tackle challenging scientific problems. Current interest includes the following:

  • New-generation exchange-correlation functionals for density functional theory (DFT) calculations
  • Time-dependent DFT for optical excitations
  • Non-equilibrium Green’s function theory for quantum charge & energy transport
  • Multiscale simulation methodology: Forcefields & QM/MM
  • Materials informatics for materials design
